Corollary 37Ando’s convexity theorem — matrix form

Let \(q, r \in \mathbb {R}\) with \(1 \leq q \leq 2\), \(r {\gt} 0\), and \(q - r {\gt} 1\), and let \(K \in M_n(\mathbb {C})\). The map

\[ (A,\, B) \; \longmapsto \; \operatorname {re}\operatorname {tr}\! \bigl(A^{-r}\, K\, B^q\, K^*\bigr) \]

Proof ▶

Apply the abstract Ando convexity theorem (Theorem 33) to \(M_n(\mathbb {C})\) with the Frobenius inner product, and use the identification \(\operatorname {re}\langle K,\, A^{-r} K B^q \rangle = \operatorname {re}\operatorname {tr}(A^{-r} K B^q K^*)\) together with the equivalences: \(A\) is strictly positive iff \(A\) is positive definite, and \(B \geq 0\) iff \(B\) is positive semidefinite.

Definition 9H\(^*\)-algebra

An H\(^*\)-algebra over \(\mathbb {k} \in \{ \mathbb {R}, \mathbb {C}\} \) is a Hilbert space \(H\) over \(\mathbb {k}\) equipped with the structure of a normed \(\mathbb {k}\)-algebra with involution \(\star \), satisfying the compatibility axioms:

\[ \langle a x,\, y \rangle = \langle x,\, a^* y \rangle \qquad \text{and} \qquad \langle x a,\, y \rangle = \langle x,\, y a^* \rangle \qquad \forall \, a, x, y \in H. \]

This notion was introduced by Ambrose  [ 1 ] .

Definition 10Left and right multiplication operators

For an H\(^*\)-algebra \(H\) and an element \(a \in H\), the left multiplication operator \(L_a : H \to H\) is the bounded linear map \(x \mapsto a x\).

The right multiplication operator \(R_a : H \to H\) is the bounded linear map \(x \mapsto x a\).

Both \(a \mapsto L_a\) and \(a \mapsto R_a\) are continuous as maps \(H \to (H \to _L H)\). Moreover \(L_a\) and \(R_b\) commute for all \(a, b \in H\): \(L_a \circ R_b = R_b \circ L_a\).

Lemma 13Spectrum of a conjugate-weighted sum

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, let \(I \subseteq \mathbb {R}\) be a convex subset, and let \(n \in \mathbb {N}\). Suppose \(a_1, \dots , a_n \in \mathcal{B}\) are self-adjoint with \(\sigma (a_i) \subseteq I\) for each \(i\), and let \(b_1, \dots , b_n \in \mathcal{B}\) satisfy \(\sum _i b_i^* b_i = 1\). Then

\[ \sigma \! \left(\sum _i b_i^*\, a_i\, b_i\right) \subseteq I. \]
Proof ▶

Set \(\alpha = \inf _i \inf \sigma (a_i)\) and \(\beta = \sup _i \sup \sigma (a_i)\); then \([\alpha , \beta ] \subseteq I\) by convexity and ord-connectedness. The unit constraint \(\sum _i b_i^* b_i = 1\) gives \(\alpha \cdot 1 \leq \sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i \leq \beta \cdot 1\) in the order on \(\mathcal{B}\), since each summand satisfies \(\alpha \cdot b_i^* b_i \leq b_i^* a_i b_i \leq \beta \cdot b_i^* b_i\). The order bounds imply the spectrum is contained in \([\alpha , \beta ]\), and hence in \(I\).

Proposition 11CFC commutativity of \(L\) and \(R\)

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(a \in H\) self-adjoint, and \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) continuous on \(\sigma (a)\). Then:

\[ L_{f(a)} = f(L_a), \qquad R_{f(a)} = f(R_a), \]

where \(f(L_a)\) and \(f(R_a)\) denote the continuous functional calculus applied to the operators \(L_a, R_a \in (H \to _L H)\).

As a consequence, for any \(r \in \mathbb {R}\):

\[ (L_a)^r\, x = a^r x, \qquad (R_a)^r\, x = x\, a^r, \]

whenever \(a \geq 0\) (for \(r \geq 0\)) or \(a\) is strictly positive (for all \(r\)).

Proof ▶

The maps \(a \mapsto L_a\) and \(a \mapsto R_a\) are star algebra homomorphisms into \((H \to _L H)\) and \((H \to _L H)^{\mathrm{op}}\) respectively, so the CFC intertwining property of star algebra homomorphisms gives \(L_{f(a)} = f(L_a)\) and \(R_{f(a)} = f(R_a)\) directly. For the \(R_a\) branch, this uses the map \(a \mapsto a^*\) as an isomorphism \(A \to A^{\mathrm{op}}\) (Proposition 1) to transport CFC from \(A\) to \(A^{\mathrm{op}}\).

Proposition 28Simplified formula for the operator power mean

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(\alpha , \beta \in \mathbb {R}\) with \(\alpha \geq 0\) and \(\beta \neq 0\). Let \(A\) be strictly positive and \(B \geq 0\). Then for all \(x \in H\),

\[ \Pi _{\alpha ,\beta }(L_A, R_B)\, x \; =\; A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^\alpha . \]
Proof ▶

The power mean is defined as \(\Pi _{\alpha ,\beta }(L_A, R_B) = ((\cdot )^\alpha \triangle (\cdot )^\beta )(R_B, L_A)\). Since \(L_A\) and \(R_B\) commute and \(R_B \geq 0\), \(L_A\) is strictly positive, the generalized perspective simplifies by Theorem 12 (which requires Lemma 14), giving \((R_B)^\alpha \cdot (L_A)^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\). Evaluating at \(x\) and using \(L_A^r\, x = A^r x\) and \(R_B^r\, x = x\, B^r\) yields \(A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^\alpha \).

Theorem 20Jensen’s operator inequality

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, \(I \subseteq \mathbb {R}\) an interval, and \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) a continuous operator convex function on \(I\). Let \(a_0, \ldots , a_n \in \mathcal{B}\) be self-adjoint elements with \(\sigma (a_i) \subseteq I\) for each \(i\), and let \(b_0, \ldots , b_n \in \mathcal{B}\) satisfy the unital condition

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i = 1. \]

Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

We follow the unitary-averaging argument of Li and Wu. Write \(m = n + 2\), set \(a_{n+1} := a_n\), and form the diagonal block element \(D = \operatorname {diag}(a_0, \ldots , a_{n+1}) \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\). Because each \(a_i\) has spectrum in \(I\), the element \(D\) is self-adjoint with spectrum in \(I\) by 13. Next, using the \(b_i\) and the constraint \(\sum b_i^* b_i = 1\), one constructs a unitary \(U \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\) whose last column is \((b_0, \ldots , b_n, 0)^T\); the remaining columns are chosen so that \(U\) is unitary, e.g. by extending \((b_0,\ldots ,b_n,0)^T\) to a unitary via a standard completion procedure. A direct computation shows that the bottom-right corner of \(U^* D U\) equals \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Now fix \(\omega = e^{2\pi i / m}\), a primitive \(m\)-th root of unity, and let \(V_k = \operatorname {diag}(1, \omega ^k, \omega ^{2k}, \ldots , \omega ^{(m-1)k})\) for \(k = 0, \ldots , m-1\). Each \(V_k\) is a unitary diagonal matrix in \(M_m(\mathcal{B})\), and the Fourier average

\[ \frac{1}{m} \sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* D U)\, V_k \]

equals the “diagonal part” of \(U^* D U\), i.e. the block-diagonal matrix whose \(j\)-th diagonal entry is the \((j,j)\)-entry of \(U^* D U\). In particular its bottom-right corner is \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Applying operator convexity of \(f\) to the averaged expression — which is a convex combination of unitarily conjugated copies of \(D\), each with spectrum in \(I\) — and then extracting the bottom-right \((1,1)\)-corner (a positive linear map that preserves the order) yields

\[ f\! \left(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\right) \; \leq \; \frac{1}{m}\sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* f(D) U)\, V_k\bigg|_{\mathrm{br}} \; =\; \sum _i b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i, \]

where the last equality uses the fact that \(f(D) = \operatorname {diag}(f(a_0),\ldots ,f(a_n),f(a_n))\) (the continuous functional calculus commutes with block-diagonal structure) and the same Fourier averaging extracts the diagonal.

Theorem 21Jensen’s operator inequality, sub-unital case

Let \(\mathcal{B}\), \(I\), \(f\), \(a_i\), and \(b_i\) be as in Theorem 20, except that the \(b_i\) now satisfy only

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i \leq 1. \]

Assume in addition that \(0 \in I\) and \(f(0) \leq 0\). Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

The left-hand side equals \(f\! \bigl(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i b_i\bigr)\) since \(a_{n+1} = 0\). The last term on the right is \(b_{n+1}^* f(0)\, b_{n+1} \leq 0\) by the assumption \(f(0) \leq 0\), so dropping it only strengthens the inequality, yielding the claimed bound.

Proof ▶

Observe that \((-f) \triangle g = -(f \triangle g)\) and that \(-f\) is operator convex with \((-f)(0) \leq 0\); apply Theorem 24 to \(-f\) and negate to obtain joint concavity of \(f \triangle g\).

Theorem 24Joint convexity of the generalized perspective function

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, and let \(f, g : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) be continuous on \([0,\infty )\). Assume:

  • \(f\) is operator convex on \([0,\infty )\) with \(f(0) \leq 0\),

  • \(g\) is operator concave on \([0,\infty )\) with \(g(x) {\gt} 0\) for all \(x {\gt} 0\).

Then the map \((L, R) \mapsto (f \triangle g)(L, R)\) is jointly convex on the set

\[ \{ (L, R) \in \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B} \mid 0 \leq L,\ R \text{ strictly positive} \} . \]
Proof ▶

Fix pairs \((L_1, R_1)\) and \((L_2, R_2)\) in the domain and weights \(a, b \geq 0\) with \(a + b = 1\). Let \(R := a R_1 + b R_2\). Define bounded operators

\[ T_1 := \bigl(a\, g(R_1)\bigr)^{1/2} g(R)^{-1/2}, \qquad T_2 := \bigl(b\, g(R_2)\bigr)^{1/2} g(R)^{-1/2}. \]

Because \(g\) is operator concave, the operator concavity inequality gives \(a\, g(R_1) + b\, g(R_2) \leq g(R)\), and therefore

\[ T_1^* T_1 + T_2^* T_2 = g(R)^{-1/2}(a\, g(R_1) + b\, g(R_2))g(R)^{-1/2} \leq \mathrm{id}. \]

That is, the pair \((T_1, T_2)\) is sub-unital in the sense required by the sub-unital Jensen inequality (Theorem 21). Applying Theorem 21 to the operator-convex function \(f\) with arguments \(G(R)^{-1/2} L_i\, G(R)^{-1/2}\) and operators \(T_i\), then conjugating by \(G(R)^{1/2}\) and collecting terms, yields

\[ a\, (f \triangle g)(L_1, R_1) + b\, (f \triangle g)(L_2, R_2) \; \geq \; (f \triangle g)(aL_1 + bL_2,\, R). \]

(The condition \(f(0) \leq 0\) absorbs the sub-unital defect term in Jensen’s inequality.) This is precisely the joint convexity inequality.

Theorem 26Joint concavity of the operator power mean
