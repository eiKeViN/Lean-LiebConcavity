Let \(q, r \in \mathbb {R}\) with \(1 \leq q \leq 2\), \(r {\gt} 0\), and \(q - r {\gt} 1\), and let \(K \in M_n(\mathbb {C})\). The map

\[ (A,\, B) \; \longmapsto \; \operatorname {re}\operatorname {tr}\! \bigl(A^{-r}\, K\, B^q\, K^*\bigr) \]

Definition 3Generalized perspective function

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra and let \(f, g : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\). The generalized perspective function \((f \triangle g) : \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{B}\) is defined by

\[ (f \triangle g)(L, R) \; :=\; g(R)^{1/2}\, f\! \left(g(R)^{-1/2}\, L\, g(R)^{-1/2}\right) g(R)^{1/2}. \]

Definition 4H\(^*\)-algebra

An H\(^*\)-algebra over \(\mathbb {k} \in \{ \mathbb {R}, \mathbb {C}\} \) is a Hilbert space \(H\) over \(\mathbb {k}\) equipped with the structure of a normed \(\mathbb {k}\)-algebra with involution \(\star \), satisfying the compatibility axioms:

\[ \langle a x,\, y \rangle = \langle x,\, a^* y \rangle \qquad \text{and} \qquad \langle x a,\, y \rangle = \langle x,\, y a^* \rangle \qquad \forall \, a, x, y \in H. \]

This notion was introduced by Ambrose  [ 1 ] .

Definition 5Left and right multiplication operators

For an H\(^*\)-algebra \(H\) and an element \(a \in H\), the left multiplication operator \(L_a : H \to H\) is the bounded linear map \(x \mapsto a x\).

The right multiplication operator \(R_a : H \to H\) is the bounded linear map \(x \mapsto x a\).

Both \(a \mapsto L_a\) and \(a \mapsto R_a\) are continuous as maps \(H \to (H \to _L H)\). Moreover \(L_a\) and \(R_b\) commute for all \(a, b \in H\): \(L_a \circ R_b = R_b \circ L_a\).

Definition 2Operator concave function

A function \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) is operator concave on \(I\) if \(-f\) is operator convex on \(I\), equivalently if \(a \mapsto f(a)\) is concave on the set of self-adjoint elements of every ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra with spectrum contained in \(I\).

Definition 1Operator convex function

A function \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) is operator convex on \(I\) if, for every ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra \(\mathcal{B}\), the map \(a \mapsto f(a)\) is convex on the set

\[ \{ a \in \mathcal{B} \mid a \text{ is self-adjoint},\ \sigma (a) \subseteq I \} , \]

where \(\sigma (a)\) denotes the spectrum of \(a\) over \(\mathbb {R}\).

Lemma 7Spectrum of a conjugate-weighted sum

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, let \(I \subseteq \mathbb {R}\) be a convex subset, and let \(n \in \mathbb {N}\). Suppose \(a_1, \dots , a_n \in \mathcal{B}\) are self-adjoint with \(\sigma (a_i) \subseteq I\) for each \(i\), and let \(b_1, \dots , b_n \in \mathcal{B}\) satisfy \(\sum _i b_i^* b_i = 1\). Then

\[ \sigma \! \left(\sum _i b_i^*\, a_i\, b_i\right) \subseteq I. \]
Proof ▶

Set \(\alpha = \inf _i \inf \sigma (a_i)\) and \(\beta = \sup _i \sup \sigma (a_i)\); then \([\alpha , \beta ] \subseteq I\) by convexity and ord-connectedness. The unit constraint \(\sum _i b_i^* b_i = 1\) gives \(\alpha \cdot 1 \leq \sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i \leq \beta \cdot 1\) in the order on \(\mathcal{B}\), since each summand satisfies \(\alpha \cdot b_i^* b_i \leq b_i^* a_i b_i \leq \beta \cdot b_i^* b_i\). The order bounds imply the spectrum is contained in \([\alpha , \beta ]\), and hence in \(I\).

Lemma 8Power of a product of commuting positives

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, let \(a, b \in \mathcal{B}\) with \(0 \leq a\), \(0 \leq b\), and \(ab = ba\). Then for all \(r \in \mathbb {R}\),

\[ (ab)^r = a^r\, b^r, \]

where the powers are taken via the continuous functional calculus.

Proof ▶

Admitted. This identity would follow from the fact that the continuous functional calculus distributes over multiplication for commuting elements. Specifically, one would need that \(\mathrm{cfc}(f, ab) = \mathrm{cfc}(f, a)\, \mathrm{cfc}(f, b)\) whenever \(a\) and \(b\) commute and \(f\) is the function \(t \mapsto t^r\). This CFC multiplicativity for commuting pairs is not yet available in Mathlib, so the lemma is currently admitted as an axiom.

Proposition 6CFC commutativity of \(L\) and \(R\)

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(a \in H\) self-adjoint, and \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) continuous on \(\sigma (a)\). Then:

\[ L_{f(a)} = f(L_a), \qquad R_{f(a)} = f(R_a), \]

where \(f(L_a)\) and \(f(R_a)\) denote the continuous functional calculus applied to the operators \(L_a, R_a \in (H \to _L H)\).

As a consequence, for any \(r \in \mathbb {R}\):

\[ (L_a)^r\, x = a^r x, \qquad (R_a)^r\, x = x\, a^r, \]

whenever \(a \geq 0\) (for \(r \geq 0\)) or \(a\) is strictly positive (for all \(r\)).

Theorem 23Ando’s Convexity Theorem

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(q \in [1,2]\), \(r {\gt} 0\) with \(q - r {\gt} 1\), and \(x \in H\). Then the map

\[ (A, B) \; \longmapsto \; \operatorname {re}\bigl\langle x,\; A^{-r}\, x\, B^{q} \bigr\rangle \]

is jointly convex on \(\{ (A, B) \in H \times H \mid A \text{ strictly positive},\, B \geq 0\} \).

Proof ▶

Set \(\alpha := q\) and \(\beta := r / (q - 1)\). The hypothesis \(q - r {\gt} 1\) with \(r {\gt} 0\) and \(q \in [1,2]\) ensures \(\beta \in (0,1]\), and one computes \(\beta (1-\alpha ) = -r\). The result follows from Theorem 20 with these parameter choices.

Theorem 20Ando convexity — general form

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(\alpha \in [1,2]\), \(\beta \in (0,1]\), and \(x \in H\). Then the map

\[ (A, B) \; \longmapsto \; \operatorname {re}\bigl\langle x,\; A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^{\alpha } \bigr\rangle \]

is jointly convex on \(\{ (A, B) \in H \times H \mid A \text{ strictly positive},\, B \geq 0\} \).

Proof ▶

The argument is analogous to Theorem 19. The same CFC commutativity identities (Proposition 6) identify the power mean evaluated at \(x\) with \(A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^\alpha \). Since \(\alpha \in [1,2]\), the power mean is jointly convex in operator order by Theorem 18, and linearity of \(T \mapsto \operatorname {re}\langle x, T x\rangle \) propagates convexity to the scalar functional.

Theorem 9Jensen’s operator inequality

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, \(I \subseteq \mathbb {R}\) an interval, and \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) a continuous operator convex function on \(I\). Let \(a_0, \ldots , a_n \in \mathcal{B}\) be self-adjoint elements with \(\sigma (a_i) \subseteq I\) for each \(i\), and let \(b_0, \ldots , b_n \in \mathcal{B}\) satisfy the unital condition

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i = 1. \]

Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

We follow the unitary-averaging argument of Li and Wu. Write \(m = n + 2\) and form the diagonal block element \(D = \operatorname {diag}(a_0, \ldots , a_n, a_n) \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\) (the last slot is a duplicate of \(a_n\), introduced to make the matrix square). Because each \(a_i\) has spectrum in \(I\), the element \(D\) is self-adjoint with spectrum in \(I\) by 7. Next, using the \(b_i\) and the constraint \(\sum b_i^* b_i = 1\), one constructs a unitary \(U \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\) whose last column is \((b_0, \ldots , b_n, 0)^T\); the remaining columns are filled in by a Gram–Schmidt procedure on the rows of \(\mathcal{B}^m\). A direct computation shows that the bottom-right corner of \(U^* D U\) equals \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Now fix \(\omega = e^{2\pi i / m}\), a primitive \(m\)-th root of unity, and let \(V_k = \operatorname {diag}(1, \omega ^k, \omega ^{2k}, \ldots , \omega ^{(m-1)k})\) for \(k = 0, \ldots , m-1\). Each \(V_k\) is a unitary diagonal matrix in \(M_m(\mathcal{B})\), and the Fourier average

\[ \frac{1}{m} \sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* D U)\, V_k \]

equals the “diagonal part” of \(U^* D U\), i.e. the block-diagonal matrix whose \(j\)-th diagonal entry is the \((j,j)\)-entry of \(U^* D U\). In particular its bottom-right corner is \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Applying operator convexity of \(f\) to the averaged expression — which is a convex combination of unitarily conjugated copies of \(D\), each with spectrum in \(I\) — and then extracting the bottom-right \((1,1)\)-corner (a positive linear map that preserves the order) yields

\[ f\! \left(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\right) \; \leq \; \frac{1}{m}\sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* f(D) U)\, V_k\bigg|_{\mathrm{br}} \; =\; \sum _i b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i, \]

where the last equality uses the fact that \(f(D) = \operatorname {diag}(f(a_0),\ldots ,f(a_n),f(a_n))\) (the continuous functional calculus commutes with block-diagonal structure) and the same Fourier averaging extracts the diagonal.

Theorem 10Jensen’s operator inequality, sub-unital case

Let \(\mathcal{B}\), \(I\), \(f\), \(a_i\), and \(b_i\) be as in Theorem 9, except that the \(b_i\) now satisfy only

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i \leq 1. \]

Assume in addition that \(0 \in I\) and \(f(0) \leq 0\). Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

Set \(b_{n+1} = \bigl(1 - \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i\bigr)^{1/2}\) and \(a_{n+1} = 0\). The extended family satisfies \(\sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^* b_i = 1\) and \(\sigma (a_{n+1}) = \{ 0\} \subseteq I\), so Theorem 9 applies to give

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]

The left-hand side equals \(f\! \bigl(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i b_i\bigr)\) since \(a_{n+1} = 0\). The last term on the right is \(b_{n+1}^* f(0)\, b_{n+1} \leq 0\) by the assumption \(f(0) \leq 0\), so dropping it only strengthens the inequality, yielding the claimed bound.

Theorem 19Lieb concavity — general form

Let \(H\) be an H\(^*\)-algebra, \(\alpha , \beta \in (0,1]\), and \(x \in H\). Then the map

\[ (A, B) \; \longmapsto \; \operatorname {re}\bigl\langle x,\; A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^{\alpha } \bigr\rangle \]

is jointly concave on \(\{ (A, B) \in H \times H \mid A \text{ strictly positive},\, B \geq 0\} \).

Proof ▶

Consider the operator power mean \(\Pi _{\alpha ,\beta }(L_A, R_B)\) acting on \(H \to _L H\), which by definition is the generalized perspective of the \(\alpha \)-power mean at the pair \((L_A, R_B)\). By the CFC commutativity of left and right multiplication (Proposition 6), we have \(L_{A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}} = (L_A)^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\) and \(R_{B^\alpha } = (R_B)^\alpha \), so the power mean evaluated at \(x\) equals \(A^{\beta (1-\alpha )}\, x\, B^\alpha \). Joint concavity of \((A, B) \mapsto \Pi _{\alpha ,\beta }(L_A, R_B)\) in the operator order on \(H \to _L H\) then follows from Theorem 17. Finally, the map \(T \mapsto \operatorname {re}\langle x, T x\rangle \) is linear and hence preserves concavity, yielding the stated result.

Theorem 15Joint convexity of the generalized perspective function

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, and let \(f, g : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) be continuous on \([0,\infty )\). Assume:

  • \(f\) is operator convex on \([0,\infty )\) with \(f(0) \leq 0\),

  • \(g\) is operator concave on \([0,\infty )\) with \(g(x) {\gt} 0\) for all \(x {\gt} 0\).

Then the map \((L, R) \mapsto (f \triangle g)(L, R)\) is jointly convex on the set

\[ \{ (L, R) \in \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{B} \mid 0 \leq L,\ R \text{ strictly positive} \} . \]
Proof ▶

Fix pairs \((L_1, R_1)\) and \((L_2, R_2)\) in the domain and weights \(a, b \geq 0\) with \(a + b = 1\). Let \(R := a R_1 + b R_2\) and write \(G := \operatorname {cfc}(g)\) for the operator \(g\) applied via the continuous functional calculus. Define bounded operators

\[ T_1 := \bigl(a\, G(R_1)\bigr)^{1/2} G(R)^{-1/2}, \qquad T_2 := \bigl(b\, G(R_2)\bigr)^{1/2} G(R)^{-1/2}. \]

Because \(g\) is operator concave, the operator concavity inequality gives \(a\, G(R_1) + b\, G(R_2) \leq G(R)\), and therefore

\[ T_1^* T_1 + T_2^* T_2 = G(R)^{-1/2}(a\, G(R_1) + b\, G(R_2))G(R)^{-1/2} \leq \mathrm{id}. \]

That is, the pair \((T_1, T_2)\) is sub-unital in the sense required by the sub-unital Jensen inequality (Theorem 10). Applying Theorem 10 to the operator-convex function \(f\) with arguments \(G(R)^{-1/2} L_i\, G(R)^{-1/2}\) and operators \(T_i\), then conjugating by \(G(R)^{1/2}\) and collecting terms, yields

\[ a\, (f \triangle g)(L_1, R_1) + b\, (f \triangle g)(L_2, R_2) \; \geq \; (f \triangle g)(aL_1 + bL_2,\, R). \]

(The condition \(f(0) \leq 0\) absorbs the sub-unital defect term in Jensen’s inequality.) This is precisely the joint convexity inequality.
