3 Jensen’s Operator Inequality

Jensen’s operator inequality is the central engine of this formalization. It generalizes the scalar Jensen inequality to the noncommutative setting: given an operator convex function \(f\) and a family of self-adjoint elements \(a_i\) in an ordered C\(^*\)-algebra, it asserts that \(f\) of any “weighted combination” of the \(a_i\) is dominated by the corresponding weighted combination of the \(f(a_i)\). In this project the inequality is applied in Chapter 5 to deduce joint convexity and concavity of the generalized perspective function, which in turn yields Lieb’s concavity theorem in Chapter 6. We follow the unitary-averaging proof of Li and Wu  [ 3 ] .

Let \(\mathcal{B}\) be an ordered unital C\(^*\)-algebra, \(I \subseteq \mathbb {R}\) an interval, and \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) a continuous operator convex function on \(I\). Let \(a_0, \ldots , a_n \in \mathcal{B}\) be self-adjoint elements with \(\sigma (a_i) \subseteq I\) for each \(i\), and let \(b_0, \ldots , b_n \in \mathcal{B}\) satisfy the unital condition

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i = 1. \]

Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

We follow the unitary-averaging argument of Li and Wu. Write \(m = n + 2\) and form the diagonal block element \(D = \operatorname {diag}(a_0, \ldots , a_n, a_n) \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\) (the last slot is a duplicate of \(a_n\), introduced to make the matrix square). Because each \(a_i\) has spectrum in \(I\), the element \(D\) is self-adjoint with spectrum in \(I\) by 7. Next, using the \(b_i\) and the constraint \(\sum b_i^* b_i = 1\), one constructs a unitary \(U \in M_m(\mathcal{B})\) whose last column is \((b_0, \ldots , b_n, 0)^T\); the remaining columns are filled in by a Gram–Schmidt procedure on the rows of \(\mathcal{B}^m\). A direct computation shows that the bottom-right corner of \(U^* D U\) equals \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Now fix \(\omega = e^{2\pi i / m}\), a primitive \(m\)-th root of unity, and let \(V_k = \operatorname {diag}(1, \omega ^k, \omega ^{2k}, \ldots , \omega ^{(m-1)k})\) for \(k = 0, \ldots , m-1\). Each \(V_k\) is a unitary diagonal matrix in \(M_m(\mathcal{B})\), and the Fourier average

\[ \frac{1}{m} \sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* D U)\, V_k \]

equals the “diagonal part” of \(U^* D U\), i.e. the block-diagonal matrix whose \(j\)-th diagonal entry is the \((j,j)\)-entry of \(U^* D U\). In particular its bottom-right corner is \(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\).

Applying operator convexity of \(f\) to the averaged expression — which is a convex combination of unitarily conjugated copies of \(D\), each with spectrum in \(I\) — and then extracting the bottom-right \((1,1)\)-corner (a positive linear map that preserves the order) yields

\[ f\! \left(\sum _i b_i^* a_i b_i\right) \; \leq \; \frac{1}{m}\sum _{k=0}^{m-1} V_k^*\, (U^* f(D) U)\, V_k\bigg|_{\mathrm{br}} \; =\; \sum _i b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i, \]

where the last equality uses the fact that \(f(D) = \operatorname {diag}(f(a_0),\ldots ,f(a_n),f(a_n))\) (the continuous functional calculus commutes with block-diagonal structure) and the same Fourier averaging extracts the diagonal.

Let \(\mathcal{B}\), \(I\), \(f\), \(a_i\), and \(b_i\) be as in Theorem 9, except that the \(b_i\) now satisfy only

\[ \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i \leq 1. \]

Assume in addition that \(0 \in I\) and \(f(0) \leq 0\). Then

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]
Proof ▶

Set \(b_{n+1} = \bigl(1 - \sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* b_i\bigr)^{1/2}\) and \(a_{n+1} = 0\). The extended family satisfies \(\sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^* b_i = 1\) and \(\sigma (a_{n+1}) = \{ 0\} \subseteq I\), so Theorem 9 applies to give

\[ f\! \left(\sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^* a_i\, b_i\right) \; \leq \; \sum _{i=0}^{n+1} b_i^*\, f(a_i)\, b_i. \]

The left-hand side equals \(f\! \bigl(\sum _{i=0}^{n} b_i^* a_i b_i\bigr)\) since \(a_{n+1} = 0\). The last term on the right is \(b_{n+1}^* f(0)\, b_{n+1} \leq 0\) by the assumption \(f(0) \leq 0\), so dropping it only strengthens the inequality, yielding the claimed bound.
